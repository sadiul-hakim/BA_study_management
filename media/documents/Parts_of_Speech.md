Most textbooks teach:

> Noun = name of a person, place, or thing.

That definition is correct, but it doesn't help much when you're reading a novel or analyzing a sentence.

A better approach is to think:

> **Parts of speech are jobs, not identities.**

A word can do different jobs in different sentences.

---

# The Most Important Rule

## A word's part of speech depends on its JOB in the sentence.

Look at this:

### Sentence 1

I bought a flower.

Here **flower** is a noun.

---

### Sentence 2

I bought a flower pot.

Here **flower** is still a noun by nature, but it is acting like an adjective because it describes what kind of pot.

What kind of pot?

→ flower pot

This is called a **noun modifier**.

English loves this pattern.

Examples:

* school bus
* football player
* coffee cup
* computer science
* chicken soup

The first noun behaves like an adjective.

---

# The Sentence Skeleton

Every English sentence has:

```text
Subject + Verb + Object
```

Example:

> I eat rice.

Subject = I

Verb = eat

Object = rice

---

# How To Find The Subject

Ask:

**Who or what performs the action?**

Example:

> Rahim plays football.

Who plays?

→ Rahim

Subject = Rahim

---

Example:

> The dog barked loudly.

Who barked?

→ dog

Subject = dog

---

# How To Find The Object

Ask:

**Who or what receives the action?**

Example:

> Rahim kicked the ball.

What was kicked?

→ ball

Object = ball

---

Example:

> I love English.

What do I love?

→ English

Object = English

---

# Predicate

Many students fear this word.

It is actually easy.

Predicate = everything except the subject.

Example:

> Rahim plays football every day.

Subject:

Rahim

Predicate:

plays football every day

---

# Verbs: The Most Important Part

You asked a very good question:

> Am is a verb?
>
> Read is a verb?
>
> Cool is a verb?

Let's see.

---

# Action Verbs

These are easiest.

Examples:

* run
* eat
* read
* write
* sleep

They show actions.

Example:

> I read books.

Read = verb

---

# Linking Verbs

These do NOT show actions.

They connect.

Examples:

* am
* is
* are
* was
* were
* seem
* become
* look

Example:

> I am happy.

Am is a verb.

But what action am I doing?

None.

Am simply connects:

I ←→ happy

---

Example:

> She is beautiful.

"is" is a verb.

No action.

Just connection.

---

# Helping Verbs

Examples:

* have
* has
* had
* do
* does
* did
* will
* can
* should

Example:

> I have finished my work.

Main verb = finished

Helping verb = have

---

Example:

> I will go.

Main verb = go

Helping verb = will

---

# How To Identify Adjectives

Forget the textbook definition.

Ask:

### Which one?

### What kind?

### How many?

---

Example:

> A red car.

What kind of car?

→ red

Adjective

---

Example:

> Three books.

How many books?

→ three

Adjective

---

Example:

> My book.

Which book?

→ my

Adjective (technically a determiner)

---

# The Big Trick

Adjectives usually modify nouns.

```text
adjective + noun
```

Examples:

* beautiful girl
* old house
* tall tree
* difficult problem

---

# How To Identify Adverbs

This is where learners struggle.

Forget "-ly" for a moment.

Ask:

### How?

### When?

### Where?

### To what extent?

---

Example:

> He runs quickly.

How does he run?

→ quickly

Adverb

---

Example:

> He arrived yesterday.

When?

→ yesterday

Adverb

---

Example:

> He lives nearby.

Where?

→ nearby

Adverb

---

# Adverbs Can Modify Adjectives

Example:

> Very beautiful.

Beautiful = adjective

Very = adverb

Why?

Because it modifies beautiful.

---

Example:

> Extremely difficult.

Difficult = adjective

Extremely = adverb

---

# One Common Confusion

### Fast

Example:

> He is a fast runner.

Fast = adjective

(It describes runner.)

---

Example:

> He runs fast.

Fast = adverb

(It describes runs.)

Same word.

Different jobs.

---

# Prepositions

These are relationship words.

Examples:

* in
* on
* at
* under
* over
* beside
* with

Example:

> The book is on the table.

On connects:

book ←→ table

---

# Conjunctions

These join things.

Examples:

* and
* but
* because
* although
* or

Example:

> I was tired, but I continued.

But joins two ideas.

---

Example:

> I stayed home because it was raining.

Because joins clauses.

---

# Interjections

Pure emotions.

Examples:

* Wow!
* Oh!
* Ouch!
* Alas!

Example:

> Wow! That's amazing.

Not really part of the sentence structure.

Just emotion.

---

# The Most Confusing Part

## Present Participle (-ing)

Example:

> The crying baby.

Crying looks like a verb.

But here it describes baby.

Therefore:

Adjective.

---

Example:

> The baby is crying.

Now crying is a verb.

Same word.

Different job.

---

# Another Confusing Part

## Verb → Noun

Example:

> I run every day.

Run = verb

---

Example:

> I went for a run.

Run = noun

---

# Adjective → Noun

Example:

> The rich are getting richer.

Normally rich is adjective.

Here:

"The rich"

means rich people.

So it acts as a noun.

---

# The Secret Used By Good Readers

When you see a word, don't ask:

> What is this word?

Ask:

> What job is this word doing right now?

Example:

> The fast train arrived quickly.

Fast → describes train → adjective

Train → noun

Arrived → verb

Quickly → describes arrived → adverb

---

# A Final Exercise

Analyze this sentence:

> The intelligent student answered the difficult question very quickly.

Ask step by step:

Who answered?

→ student (subject)

What kind of student?

→ intelligent (adjective)

What did he answer?

→ question (object)

What kind of question?

→ difficult (adjective)

How did he answer?

→ quickly (adverb)

How quickly?

→ very quickly

(very = adverb modifying another adverb)

This way of asking questions ("who?", "what?", "which?", "what kind?", "how?", "when?", "where?") is far more useful than memorizing definitions. It is also how many experienced readers unconsciously analyze sentences while reading novels, essays, and newspapers.

## The Three Most Confusing Patterns in English

### 1. Noun → Adjective

* coffee cup
* flower pot
* school bus
* chicken soup

The first noun modifies the second noun.

---

### 2. Verb → Adjective

* sleeping baby
* broken window
* running water
* excited students

---

### 3. Adjective → Noun

* the rich
* the poor
* the blind
* the elderly

---

If you can correctly analyze 40–50 of these without looking at the answers, your understanding of parts of speech will be stronger than that of many students who have memorized grammar definitions for years.

# Paragraph 1

The **young** engineer arrived at the office very early. He **carefully** checked the design before the meeting. His team had spent several weeks solving a **difficult** problem. During the discussion, the manager asked many questions, but the engineer answered them **confidently**. Everyone appreciated his work. After the meeting, he returned to his desk and continued working on a new software project. Although the deadline was near, nobody seemed worried because the team had already completed most of the important tasks.

### Answers

* **young** → Adjective (describes engineer)
* **carefully** → Adverb (modifies checked)
* **difficult** → Adjective (describes problem)
* **confidently** → Adverb (modifies answered)

---

# Paragraph 2

A **sleeping** dog lay beside the road while people walked past it. A small boy stopped and looked at the animal **curiously**. The dog suddenly opened its eyes and stood up. Fortunately, it was **friendly** and simply wagged its tail. The boy smiled and offered it some food. After eating, the dog followed the boy for a short distance before turning back toward the market. The scene was so **peaceful** that several people paused to watch it for a few moments.

### Answers

* **sleeping** → Adjective (describes dog)
* **curiously** → Adverb (modifies looked)
* **friendly** → Adjective (describes dog)
* **peaceful** → Adjective (describes scene)

# Paragraph 3

The village school organized a science fair on Friday. Students from different classes presented their projects. One group built a **wood** bridge model, while another created a **working** robot. The judges listened **attentively** as the students explained their ideas. Many parents attended the event and expressed **great** pride in their children's achievements. By the end of the day, the school hall was full of excitement and laughter. Everyone agreed that the fair had been a remarkable success.

### Answers

* **wood** → Noun acting as an adjective (noun modifier; describes bridge)
* **working** → Adjective (describes robot)
* **attentively** → Adverb (modifies listened)
* **great** → Adjective (describes pride)

# Paragraph 4

Although the weather was extremely hot, the hikers continued their journey. They walked **slowly** through the forest and carried enough water for the trip. One hiker noticed a **fallen** tree blocking the path. The group discussed the situation and eventually found a **safe** route around the obstacle. As evening approached, the sky became **beautifully** orange and red. The hikers reached their destination just before dark and celebrated with a simple meal beside the river.

### Answers

* **slowly** → Adverb (modifies walked)
* **fallen** → Adjective (describes tree)
* **safe** → Adjective (describes route)
* **beautifully** → Adverb (modifies orange/red appearance; describes how the sky became colored)

# Paragraph 5

The professor entered the classroom and began an **interesting** lecture about language. He explained how some words can change their grammatical function depending on the sentence. For example, in "flower pot," the word flower acts as a **modifier**, while in "the flower is beautiful," it is a noun. The students listened **closely** and took **careful** notes. Several students asked questions, and the professor responded **patiently**. By the end of the class, everyone had gained a deeper understanding of English grammar.

### Answers

* **interesting** → Adjective (describes lecture)
* **modifier** → Noun
* **closely** → Adverb (modifies listened)
* **careful** → Adjective (describes notes)
* **patiently** → Adverb (modifies responded)
