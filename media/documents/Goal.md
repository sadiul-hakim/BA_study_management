Based on your goal (**speaking better, reading literature, improving vocabulary, and becoming comfortable in English conversations**), I would study grammar in roughly this order:

## 1. Tenses ✓

You've already started this.

---

## 2. Conditionals ✓

You've just learned these.

---

## 3. Modal Verbs ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

This is probably the next most useful topic.

You hear them everywhere:

* can
* could
* may
* might
* must
* should
* would
* ought to

Examples:

* Can you help me?
* You should study more.
* I might go tomorrow.
* I would like some tea.

Many learners know tenses but sound unnatural because they don't use modal verbs well.

---

## 4. Prepositions ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

A huge source of mistakes.

Examples:

* interested **in**
* good **at**
* afraid **of**
* depend **on**
* listen **to**

Examples:

❌ I am good in English.
✅ I am good at English.

❌ Listen me.
✅ Listen to me.

---

## 5. Relative Clauses ⭐⭐⭐⭐

They help you combine sentences naturally.

Instead of:

> I met a man. He helped me.

Say:

> I met a man who helped me.

Words:

* who
* whom
* whose
* which
* that

---

## 6. Passive Voice ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Very common in books, news, articles, and formal writing.

Active:

> Shakespeare wrote the play.

Passive:

> The play was written by Shakespeare.

You'll encounter passive voice constantly while reading.

---

## 7. Reported Speech ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Used when telling someone what another person said.

Direct:

> He said, "I am busy."

Reported:

> He said that he was busy.

Very common in storytelling and conversation.

---

## 8. Articles (a, an, the) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Even advanced learners struggle with these.

* a book
* an apple
* the sun

Understanding **the** properly is especially important.

---

## 9. Gerunds and Infinitives ⭐⭐⭐⭐

One of the most common advanced topics.

Examples:

* I enjoy reading.
* I want to read.
* I like swimming.
* I hope to travel.

Notice how some verbs take **-ing** and others take **to + verb**.

---

## 10. Phrasal Verbs ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

This is where spoken English becomes natural.

Examples:

* wake up
* give up
* look after
* turn on
* turn off
* find out

Native speakers use these constantly.

Example:

Instead of:

> I discovered the truth.

People often say:

> I found out the truth.

---

# If I were teaching you personally

Considering your current level, I would follow this path:

1. Tenses
2. Conditionals
3. Modal Verbs
4. Prepositions
5. Articles
6. Relative Clauses
7. Passive Voice
8. Reported Speech
9. Gerunds & Infinitives
10. Phrasal Verbs

After that, I would spend **more time reading and listening than studying grammar**, because at your level the biggest gains come from exposure, not from memorizing more grammar rules.

In fact, if you read simple novels and stories regularly, you'll naturally absorb most of these grammar patterns without consciously studying every rule.
