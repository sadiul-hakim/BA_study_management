These are not one single grammar category. They belong to a broader area that English grammar books call verb patterns or sentence patterns. Native speakers don't usually think about the names—they simply learn the patterns.

---

## 1. **be + to + verb**

This expresses **official plans, duty, instructions, or destiny**.

Examples:

* I **am to** meet the manager tomorrow.

  * আগামীকাল আমার ম্যানেজারের সঙ্গে দেখা করার কথা।
* The President **is to** visit Japan next week.

  * রাষ্ট্রপতির আগামী সপ্তাহে জাপান সফর করার কথা।

This is common in **news reports**, **formal speech**, and **official instructions**, but less common in casual conversation.

In everyday speech, people usually say:

> I'm going to meet the manager tomorrow.

---

## 2. **wish + to + verb**

This means **want to**, but it is **more formal and polite**.

Example:

* I **wish to** speak to the manager.

  * আমি ম্যানেজারের সঙ্গে কথা বলতে চাই।

In everyday conversation, native speakers usually say:

> I want to speak...
>
> I'd like to speak...

---

## 3. **cannot help + verb-ing**

This means **cannot stop oneself from doing something**.

Examples:

* I **can't help laughing**.

  * আমি হাসি আটকাতে পারি না।
* She **couldn't help crying**.

  * সে কান্না থামাতে পারেনি।

This is very common in both speaking and writing.

---

## 4. **cannot but + base verb**

This means **have no choice but to**.

Example:

* I **cannot but admire** him.

  * আমি তাকে প্রশংসা না করে পারি না।

This structure is **old-fashioned and formal**.

Modern English usually says:

* I **can't help admiring** him.
* I **have no choice but to admire** him.
