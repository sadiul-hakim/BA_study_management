John Keats (1795–1821) was one of the greatest Romantic poets. His poems are not stories like novels. Instead, they express **feelings, beauty, imagination, nature, life, death, and art**. His language can be difficult, but the ideas become much easier when you understand the scene first.

Let's go through each poem in simple English.

---

# 1. Ode on Melancholy

## Main Theme

**Sadness (melancholy) is a natural part of happiness.**

Keats says that we should not try to escape sadness because sadness always comes with beauty and joy.

---

## Scene

Imagine a beautiful spring morning.

The flowers are blooming.

The rainbow appears after rain.

Someone you love smiles at you.

Everything looks wonderful.

But Keats says:

> "These beautiful things won't last forever."

Flowers die.

The rainbow disappears.

Youth becomes old.

Love changes.

Because beautiful things disappear, they make us feel sad.

That sadness is called **melancholy**.

---

## Story

There isn't a real story.

The speaker is talking directly to the reader.

He first tells us what **NOT** to do.

When you become sad:

❌ Don't poison yourself.

❌ Don't think about death.

❌ Don't hide your feelings.

Instead...

Go outside.

Look at flowers.

Watch the clouds.

Enjoy your lover's beauty.

Because happiness and sadness always live together.

---

## Characters

Only a few symbolic characters appear.

### The Speaker

John Keats himself (or the poetic speaker).

He gives advice.

---

### Melancholy

Not a real woman.

She is imagined like a goddess.

She lives inside beautiful things.

Whenever beauty disappears, Melancholy appears.

---

### Beauty

Beauty is also imagined like a goddess.

She cannot stay forever.

---

## Main Idea

The happier something is...

the sadder we become when it ends.

Therefore,

**Beauty creates sadness.**

---

# 2. Ode on a Grecian Urn

## Main Theme

**Art lasts forever, but human life does not.**

---

## Scene

Imagine an ancient Greek vase (urn).

It has pictures carved on it.

The pictures never move.

Everything stays frozen forever.

Keats looks carefully at those pictures.

---

## Story

The poem has no real story.

Keats walks around the urn and imagines the stories shown on it.

---

### First Picture

A young man is chasing a beautiful girl.

He never catches her.

The girl keeps running forever.

They never grow old.

---

### Second Picture

A young musician is playing a flute.

We cannot hear the music.

But Keats says:

"The music inside imagination is even sweeter."

---

### Third Picture

A young lover is about to kiss his beloved.

But...

He never kisses her.

He remains just before the kiss forever.

---

### Fourth Picture

People are taking a cow to sacrifice to the gods.

Everyone leaves a small town.

The town becomes empty forever.

---

## Characters

### The Speaker

John Keats.

---

### Young Lover

Always young.

Never becomes old.

Never kisses the girl.

---

### Beautiful Girl

Always beautiful.

Never ages.

---

### Musician

Always playing.

Never stops.

---

### Priests

Taking an animal for sacrifice.

---

### Villagers

Leaving the town.

---

## Main Idea

Humans

✔ grow old

✔ die

✔ lose beauty

But art

✔ never changes

✔ never grows old

✔ lives forever

The famous last line means:

> "Beauty is truth, truth beauty."

Keats suggests that beauty itself holds a kind of truth, even if we cannot fully explain it.

---

# 3. Ode to a Nightingale

This is Keats' most famous poem.

---

## Main Theme

**People suffer and die, but the beauty of nature continues forever.**

---

## Scene

One evening...

Keats hears a nightingale singing.

The bird sings happily.

Keats feels sad.

He wishes he could escape the painful human world.

---

## Story

### Beginning

Keats is unhappy.

His heart feels heavy.

Not because he hates the bird.

Because the bird is so happy.

---

### Then

He wishes he could drink wine.

Why?

To forget the world.

The world has:

Old age.

Disease.

Pain.

Death.

---

### Next

Instead of wine,

he uses imagination.

His imagination flies with the bird.

---

### Then

He imagines living in the bird's world.

There is:

Beautiful forest.

Moonlight.

Flowers.

Sweet smell.

Music.

Peace.

---

### Then

He even says:

"If I die now...

while listening to this beautiful song...

it would be a beautiful death."

---

### Finally

The bird flies away.

The song disappears.

Keats wakes from his imagination.

He wonders:

Was it a dream?

Or was it real?

---

## Characters

### The Speaker

John Keats.

---

### Nightingale

Not an ordinary bird.

It symbolizes:

Immortality

Nature

Joy

Poetry

---

### Death

Death is imagined as something peaceful.

Not frightening.

---

## Main Idea

Humans suffer.

Nature keeps singing.

People die.

The bird's song continues generation after generation.

---

# 4. On First Looking into Chapman's Homer

## Background

Keats had never read Homer's works in a translation that truly inspired him.

Then he read **George Chapman's** English translation.

He became amazed.

---

## Main Theme

**The excitement of discovering something wonderful for the first time.**

---

## Scene

Keats finishes reading Chapman's translation.

He feels as if he has discovered a completely new world.

---

## Story

Keats compares his feeling to two explorers.

---

### First Comparison

A scientist discovers a new planet.

Imagine looking into the sky...

and suddenly seeing a new star.

That is Keats' excitement.

---

### Second Comparison

He compares himself to the Spanish explorer **Hernán Cortés** (though historically it was **Vasco Núñez de Balboa** who first saw the Pacific Ocean from the Americas).

Imagine climbing a mountain.

You reach the top.

Suddenly...

you see the huge Pacific Ocean for the first time.

Everyone becomes silent because the sight is so amazing.

That is exactly how Keats felt after reading Chapman.

---

## Characters

### John Keats

The speaker.

---

### Homer

The great ancient Greek poet whose works inspired Keats.

---

### George Chapman

The English translator whose version opened a new world for Keats.

---

### Cortés (mentioned in the poem)

Used as the explorer in Keats's comparison.

---

## Main Idea

Reading a great book can feel like discovering a new continent.

---

# Quick Revision Table

| Poem                                      | Main Theme                                                   | Main Scene                                         | Important Characters                             |
| ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| **Ode on Melancholy**                     | Happiness and sadness always exist together                  | Looking at beautiful things that will fade         | Speaker, Melancholy, Beauty                      |
| **Ode on a Grecian Urn**                  | Art is permanent; human life is temporary                    | An ancient Greek urn with frozen scenes            | Speaker, Lover, Girl, Musician, Priests          |
| **Ode to a Nightingale**                  | Nature is everlasting; humans suffer and die                 | Keats listens to a nightingale singing in a forest | Speaker, Nightingale, Death (symbolic)           |
| **On First Looking into Chapman's Homer** | Discovering great literature is like discovering a new world | Keats reads Chapman's translation of Homer         | Keats, Homer, George Chapman, Cortés (mentioned) |

## A simple trick to remember them

* **Ode on Melancholy** → *Beauty fades, so sadness is natural.*
* **Ode on a Grecian Urn** → *Art never grows old, but people do.*
* **Ode to a Nightingale** → *The bird keeps singing while human life is full of suffering and ends in death.*
* **On First Looking into Chapman's Homer** → *Reading a great translation feels like discovering a new world.*
