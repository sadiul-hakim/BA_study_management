One thing I want to say before we start:

> **Passive voice is much more common in writing than in speaking.**

In everyday conversation, native speakers generally prefer the **active voice** because it is shorter, clearer, and more direct.

Compare:

**Active (more natural in speech)**

> Somebody stole my bike.

**Passive (also correct)**

> My bike was stolen.

Both are common, but the first is more conversational when the doer matters. The second is common when the bike (not the thief) is the focus.

---

# What is Passive Voice?

Forget grammar definitions.

Think like this:

Every action has two sides.

```text
Rahim kicked the ball.
```

Who did the action?

→ Rahim

Who received the action?

→ the ball

Active voice talks about **the doer**.

Passive voice talks about **the receiver**.

---

# The Biggest Trick

Ask yourself:

## "Who is more important in this sentence?"

### Example 1

The chef cooked the food.

Who is important?

The chef.

Active voice.

---

### Example 2

The food was cooked by the chef.

Now the food is important.

Passive voice.

---

# Formula (Easy Version)

Instead of memorizing twenty rules, remember only this:

```text
Object
↓

be verb
+

Past Participle (Verb₃)
```

Examples

Active

> Rahim writes a letter.

Passive

> A letter is written by Rahim.

Notice:

writes

↓

is written

---

Active

> They built the bridge.

Passive

> The bridge was built.

---

The only thing that really changes is the **be verb**.

---

# How to Convert Active → Passive

Step 1

Find the object.

Example

> The police arrested the thief.

Object?

→ the thief

---

Step 2

Move it to the front.

The thief...

---

Step 3

Choose the correct be verb.

Past tense

↓

was

---

Step 4

Use Verb₃.

arrest

↓

arrested

Final:

> The thief was arrested.

# Why Do Native Speakers Use Passive?

There are four major reasons.

---

## 1. The Doer is Unknown

You don't know who did it.

Example

> My phone was stolen.

You don't know the thief.

Very common.

---

Example

> My window was broken.

Who broke it?

Unknown.

---

## 2. The Doer Doesn't Matter

Example

> English is spoken worldwide.

Who speaks it?

Millions of people.

Not important.

---

Example

> Rice is grown in Bangladesh.

The farmers are not the focus.

Rice is.

---

## 3. Formal Writing

News

> Three people were injured.

Research

> The experiment was conducted in 2024.

Business

> Your application has been approved.

Passive voice sounds more formal and objective.

---

## 4. To Be Polite

Instead of

> You made a mistake.

People often say

> A mistake was made.

Notice how the second sentence sounds softer because it doesn't directly blame someone.

---

# When NOT to Use Passive

This is more important than learning the rules.

---

Don't use passive when the doer matters.

Bad:

> The goal was scored by Messi.

Natural in conversation:

> Messi scored the goal.

---

Bad:

> The novel was written by George Orwell.

Better in conversation:

> George Orwell wrote the novel.

---

Bad:

> The dinner was cooked by my mother.

Better:

> My mother cooked dinner.

---

# Speaking Rule

If the person doing the action is important,

use Active.

If the result is important,

use Passive.

---

# The Most Common Passive Sentences

Native speakers use these constantly.

---

The door is closed.

Nobody cares who closed it.

---

The shop is open.

Not:

Someone opened the shop.

---

The meeting has been canceled.

Who canceled it?

Doesn't matter.

---

The road is blocked.

Who blocked it?

Unknown.

---

The flight was delayed.

Who delayed it?

Not important.

---

The package has been delivered.

Again,

the package is important.

---

# Passive Without "by"

Beginners think every passive sentence needs "by."

No.

Actually,

most passive sentences do NOT use "by."

Examples

> My wallet was stolen.

NOT

> My wallet was stolen by someone.

---

> The meeting was canceled.

NOT

> The meeting was canceled by the manager.

Unless the manager is important.

---

# Passive in Different Tenses

Instead of memorizing twelve tables,

see the pattern.

Present

> The cake is made.

Past

> The cake was made.

Future

> The cake will be made.

Present Perfect

> The cake has been made.

See the pattern?

Only the **be verb** changes.

The last verb always stays

```text
Verb₃
```

# Confusing Part

## Get Passive

Very common in speech.

Instead of

> I was fired.

People often say

> I got fired.

Instead of

> He was injured.

People often say

> He got injured.

Instead of

> We were invited.

People often say

> We got invited.

This is extremely common in American English and informal conversation.

---

# Another Confusing Part

Some adjectives look like passive verbs.

Example

> The door is closed.

Is "closed" an adjective?

Or a passive verb?

Sometimes,

both interpretations make sense.

Another example

> I am interested in English.

Interested originally comes from a verb (interest).

But today,

we usually think of **interested** as an adjective.

The same happens with:

* excited
* tired
* bored
* surprised
* worried
* married

Example

> I am tired.

"Tired" behaves like an adjective, even though it comes from the verb "tire."

---

# Verbs That Cannot Be Passive

Only verbs with an object can become passive.

Active:

> She opened the door.

Passive:

> The door was opened.

Correct.

---

But:

> He slept.

Object?

None.

You cannot say:

> ❌ Was slept.

---

Another example:

> She arrived.

No object.

No passive.

---

# Memory Trick

Before making a passive sentence, ask:

**Can I answer the question "What?" or "Whom?" after the verb?**

* She opened **what?** → the door ✔️ (Passive is possible.)
* He kicked **what?** → the ball ✔️
* They built **what?** → a bridge ✔️

But:

* He slept **what?** ❌
* She arrived **what?** ❌
* They laughed **what?** ❌

No object means no passive voice.

# The Three Passive Sentences You Will Hear All the Time

1. **It's called...**

> What is this called?

বাংলা: **এটাকে কী বলা হয়?**

Notice that we don't usually ask, "What do people call this?" in everyday conversation. The passive form is more natural.

---

2. **I'm interested in...**

> I'm interested in history.

বাংলা: **আমি ইতিহাসে আগ্রহী।**

Although it looks like a passive construction, **interested** functions as an adjective in modern English.

---

3. **I got promoted.**

> I got promoted last month.

বাংলা: **গত মাসে আমার পদোন্নতি হয়েছে।**

This **get-passive** is extremely common in spoken English.

---

## My advice for improving your speaking

Don't try to turn every active sentence into a passive one. Instead, learn the **fixed passive expressions** that native speakers use naturally:

* It's called...
* I'm done.
* I'm finished.
* I'm married.
* I'm interested in...
* I'm worried about...
* I got hired.
* I got promoted.
* I got accepted.
* It was canceled.
* It's made of...
* It's known for...
* It's located in...

These expressions appear constantly in everyday conversations. Once you're comfortable with them, passive voice will start to feel natural rather than like a grammar exercise.
