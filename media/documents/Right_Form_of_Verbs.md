The topic is usually called **Subject-Verb Agreement**, but for speaking, I think of it differently:

> **The verb follows the grammatical subject, not the meaning.**

This is why learners get confused.

For example:

> Everyone **is** happy.

Your brain says:

"Everyone means many people."

But English grammar says:

"Everyone" is **grammatically singular**, so we use **is**.

This is one of the biggest differences between English and Bangla.

---

# Step 1: The Easy Subjects

| Subject | Verb            |
| ------- | --------------- |
| I       | am / go / have  |
| You     | are / go / have |
| We      | are / go / have |
| They    | are / go / have |
| He      | is / goes / has |
| She     | is / goes / has |
| It      | is / goes / has |

You already know these.

---

# Step 2: The "One Person" Words

These confuse almost everyone.

Although they talk about many people, English treats them as **one unit**.

Always use a **singular verb**.

| Subject    | Verb |
| ---------- | ---- |
| Everyone   | is   |
| Everybody  | is   |
| Someone    | is   |
| Somebody   | is   |
| Anyone     | is   |
| Anybody    | is   |
| Nobody     | is   |
| No one     | is   |
| Everything | is   |
| Something  | is   |
| Nothing    | is   |
| Each       | is   |
| Either     | is   |
| Neither    | is   |

Examples:

✅ Everyone **likes** coffee.

সবাই কফি পছন্দ করে।

---

✅ Everybody **knows** him.

সবাই তাকে চেনে।

---

✅ Someone **is** waiting outside.

কেউ একজন বাইরে অপেক্ষা করছে।

---

✅ Nobody **has** called me.

কেউ আমাকে ফোন করেনি।

---

✅ Everything **looks** perfect.

সবকিছু নিখুঁত দেখাচ্ছে।

---

# Memory Trick

If the word ends in:

* one
* body
* thing

it is almost always **singular**.

Examples:

* someone
* everybody
* nothing
* anything

All singular.

---

# Step 3: None

This one is tricky.

Both are acceptable.

### Singular

None of the money **is** missing.

Money is uncountable.

---

### Plural

None of the students **are** absent.

Very common in speaking.

---

Modern English often allows **plural** after "none of + plural noun."

---

# Step 4: Some / Any

These depend on the noun.

### Countable

Some students **are** here.

---

Some books **are** missing.

---

### Uncountable

Some water **is** left.

---

Some information **is** useful.

---

The verb follows the noun after "some."

---

# Step 5: All

Again,

depends on the noun.

All the students **are** ready.

---

All the milk **is** gone.

---

# Step 6: Most

Most people **are** friendly.

---

Most of the cake **is** gone.

---

Again,

look at the noun.

---

# Step 7: Who

Who takes the verb of the person it refers to.

Who **is** there?

---

Who **wants** coffee?

Unknown person

↓

Singular

---

Who **are** those people?

Now it refers to several people.

Plural.

---

# Step 8: Which

Depends.

Which book **is** yours?

---

Which books **are** yours?

---

# Step 9: What

Usually singular.

What **is** your name?

---

What **was** that?

---

But:

What **are** your plans?

Because "plans" is plural.

---

# Step 10: There is / There are

Don't look at "there."

Look after the verb.

There **is** a book.

---

There **are** two books.

---

There **is** some water.

---

# Step 11: Every

Always singular.

Every student **has** a notebook.

---

Every child **needs** love.

---

Never

❌ Every students are...

---

# Step 12: Each

Always singular.

Each student **receives** a prize.

---

Each of the boys **has** a bicycle.

Notice

"Boys" is plural,

but the subject is

Each.

---

# Step 13: Either / Neither

Always singular.

Either answer **is** correct.

---

Neither student **was** late.

---

# Step 14: One of...

This confuses many learners.

One of the students **is** absent.

Why?

Subject:

One

Not

students.

---

# Step 15: More Than One

Looks plural.

Actually singular.

More than one student **has** failed.

Not

have.

---

# Step 16: A Number Of vs The Number Of

Very common in newspapers.

A number of students **are** absent.

Means:

Many students.

Plural.

---

The number of students **is** increasing.

Subject:

The number

Singular.

---

# Step 17: Collective Nouns

Words like:

* family
* team
* class
* committee
* government

American English

Usually singular.

The team **is** winning.

---

British English

Often plural.

The team **are** playing well.

Both are correct depending on the variety of English.

For speaking with international learners, using the **singular** is a safe choice.

---

# Step 18: News, Mathematics, Politics

They end in **-s** but are singular.

Mathematics **is** difficult.

---

Physics **is** interesting.

---

News **is** good.

Not

News are.

---

# Step 19: Police, People

These are plural.

The police **are** investigating.

---

People **are** friendly.

Never

People is.

---

# Step 20: Money, Furniture, Information

Uncountable.

The information **is** useful.

---

The furniture **is** expensive.

---

The advice **is** helpful.

Never

❌ informations

❌ furnitures

❌ advices

---

# Step 21: Both

Always plural.

Both students **are** ready.

---

Both of them **have** arrived.

---

# Step 22: Either...or / Neither...nor

The verb usually agrees with the subject **closest** to it.

Either my brother or my parents **are** coming.

Closest subject:

parents

↓

Plural

---

Either my parents or my brother **is** coming.

Closest subject:

brother

↓

Singular

This is called the **proximity rule**, and even native speakers rely on it.

# The Habit Fluent Speakers Develop

Instead of looking at the whole sentence, train yourself to ask:

> **"What is the real subject?"**

For example:

> **One of the books is missing.**

Your eyes will naturally be drawn to **books**, but the real subject is **one**, so the verb is **is**.

Another example:

> **Each of the players has a locker.**

The noun **players** is plural, but the real subject is **each**, so we use **has**.
